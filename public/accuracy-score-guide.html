Understanding Accuracy Scores

Learn how Verity calculates confidence scores and what they mean for your fact-checking results

üìä Confidence Scale Explained

Our confidence scores range from 0% to 100% and indicate how strongly evidence supports or contradicts a claim. Here's what each range means:

80-100%
Highly Verified

Strong agreement across all sources and AI providers. Multiple independent confirmations.

60-80%
Likely True

Good evidence supporting the claim with minimal dissenting sources.

40-60%
Disputed/Unclear

Mixed evidence or conflicting sources. Requires additional context.

0-40%
Likely False

Strong evidence contradicting the claim or unable to be verified.

üí° Important: A confidence score is NOT the same as accuracy. It reflects consensus strength from our sources and AI providers, not absolute truth.

‚öôÔ∏è How We Calculate Scores

Our confidence score is calculated using a weighted combination of four key factors:

Confidence Score = (AI Provider Agreement √ó 0.35) + (Source Credibility √ó 0.30) + (Evidence Strength √ó 0.20) + (Consensus Score √ó 0.15)

Component Breakdown

1. AI Provider Agreement (35% weight)
We query 14+ AI models (Claude, GPT-4, Llama, Mistral, etc.) with the same claim. This factor measures how many agree on the verdict.
Example: If 12 out of 14 models agree it's true = 85.7% agreement
2. Source Credibility (30% weight)
Not all sources are equal. We rank sources by authority and reliability. Peer-reviewed journals carry more weight than blog posts.
Example: Finding the claim in USGS, Nature, and NASA = high credibility (95%)
3. Evidence Strength (20% weight)
Measures the quantity and quality of supporting or contradicting evidence. More sources and stronger evidence = higher strength.
Example: One source vs. ten converging sources = Different strength scores
4. Consensus Score (15% weight)
General agreement across the fact-checking community (academic, journalistic, scientific). Strong consensus boosts confidence; major disagreements lower it.
Example: "Earth orbits the Sun" has near-universal consensus = 99%+

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

What does a 75% confidence score mean?
It means there's good evidence supporting the claim, with strong agreement from sources and AI models. Most likely true, but there may be some dissenting sources or edge cases to consider. In the "Likely True" category (60-80%).
Why do similar claims have different scores?
Context matters. "Vitamin C prevents colds" might score differently than "Vitamin C helps immune function." The exact wording, available sources, and scientific consensus affect the score. Our system analyzes the specific claim as written.
Can a 50% confidence claim become true or false?
Yes. As new evidence emerges, scores can change. For disputed claims, additional context (time period, location, circumstances) can shift the verdict. We update scores as new research appears.
Why should I trust these scores?
Our scores are calculated transparently using multiple independent AI providers and credible sources. We show you why we reached each conclusion with specific citations. You can verify our reasoning yourself using the provided sources.
What if I disagree with the score?
You should! Please review the sources and reasoning we provide. If you find better evidence, contact us. Fact-checking is collaborative, and new information should update the consensus.
Are older claims scored differently?
Potentially, yes. Claims about recent events or new research may have lower confidence (limited evidence) while historical facts may have higher confidence (established record). The temporal context is factored into our analysis.
How do you handle opinions vs. facts?
We focus on factual claims. Opinions ("The best pizza is Neapolitan") are inherently subjective and cannot be fact-checked. We'll flag subjective claims and explain why they're not verifiable.
What does "Unverifiable" mean?
A claim that cannot be proven true or false with available evidence. Example: "I had eggs for breakfast yesterday" - we have no way to verify this. It's not false, just not verifiable by our system.

‚ö° Important Notes

‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations: Our confidence scores represent consensus from available sources, not absolute truth. Edge cases, future discoveries, or context we're unaware of may change verdicts. Always consider multiple sources and expert opinion for critical decisions.
üí° Best Practices:
  • Review the sources and reasoning we provide
  • Check the dates on sources (recent? outdated?)
  • Consider context (when? where? who says?)
  • Cross-reference with other fact-checkers
  • Contact us if you find errors or new evidence
üî¨ Our Approach:

We believe in transparency over speed. Rather than giving you quick answers, we show you detailed reasoning, actual sources, and AI provider consensus. You can verify our work and understand how we reached each conclusion.
